# Population Migration and Urban Form: European Evidence 2010–2022

A spatial analysis of internal and international migration flows across European
metropolitan areas, combining Eurostat population registers, administrative
records, and — for the 2020–2022 period — anonymised mobile network operator
datasets to overcome the lag inherent in traditional census-based measurement.
The study covers 68 metropolitan areas (NUTS-2 level) and 12 years of
comparable data.

---

## Regional Trends

### Origin and Destination Patterns

Net migration flows are strongly directed toward four major metropolitan clusters:
the Randstad (Netherlands), Greater London (UK), the Paris Basin (France), and
the Rhine-Ruhr agglomeration (Germany). Together these absorbed **61 % of all
net intra-EU migrants** between 2015 and 2022, despite containing only 18 % of
the EU population — a concentration ratio of 3.4. ^migration-flows

Origin regions are predominantly rural areas of central and eastern Europe,
particularly in Bulgaria, Romania, and Poland, where continued rural depopulation
feeds a self-reinforcing outflow: as agricultural employment contracts and social
infrastructure in villages deteriorates, out-migration accelerates.

### Secondary City Growth

A counter-trend of secondary city growth has emerged strongly post-2020, with
cities of 200 000–500 000 inhabitants recording the highest *proportional*
population gains in the sample — averaging **+2.1 % per year** compared to
+0.8 % for primary cities above one million inhabitants. Remote-work adoption is
the leading explanatory variable, with a unit increase in the share of
remote-compatible jobs in a city's employment base associated with a 0.47
percentage-point increase in annual net in-migration. ^secondary-city-growth

These secondary cities typically lack the metro-grade transit infrastructure of
primary cities. Rapid population growth without commensurate transit investment
risks locking new residents into car dependence before behavioural habits form.

### Seasonal and Cyclical Patterns

Short-term mobility (stays of 3–12 months) has grown disproportionately, driven
by the expansion of remote work and cross-border employment contracts. Standard
annual migration statistics undercount this population by an estimated **22–35 %**,
creating a systematic underestimate of transit demand in receiving cities,
particularly for off-peak services used for non-commute trips.

## Urban Attractors

### Labour Market Access

Proximity to employment concentrations remains the dominant factor in residential
location choice, explaining 54 % of variance in destination selection in
regression models that control for housing cost, amenity quality, and social
network proximity. The estimated premium for a 10-minute reduction in commute
time to the city's employment core is equivalent to a **€45 000 reduction in
housing cost** in the median metropolitan market. ^labour-market-premium

### Transit Network Quality

Access to a high-frequency public transit stop (service interval ≤ 10 minutes)
within 400 m raises a neighbourhood's attractiveness to incoming migrants by
**18 % on average**, net of employment access and housing cost. This effect is
identified through a quasi-experimental design exploiting the staggered rollout
of metro extensions across five cities between 2010 and 2020. ^transit-attractiveness

The effect creates a reinforcing cycle: population growth justifies transit
investment, transit investment attracts further population growth, and the new
residents generate tax revenue that funds subsequent transit expansions. Cities
that broke into this virtuous cycle through one large transit investment have
sustained above-average migration attractiveness for the subsequent decade.

### Housing Cost Constraints

Rising housing costs in primary cities progressively deflect incoming migrants
toward inner suburbs and secondary cities, generating demand for improved
orbital (cross-suburb) transit links that most legacy radial networks do not
provide. In the four primary clusters studied, average house prices have risen
by **127 % in real terms** since 2010, pushing the median new migrant household
35 % further from the city centre than a decade ago.

---

## Implications for Transit Planning

Migration-driven population growth concentrates in corridors near existing
transit nodes, making targeted capacity expansions along those corridors more
efficient than network extensions into low-density areas. However, the corridors
that absorb growth today are not necessarily those that will absorb it over the
next decade: secondary-city growth and housing-cost-driven dispersal are
continuously shifting the demand landscape.

Planners should track migration flows at **quarterly rather than annual**
resolution to identify emerging hotspots before infrastructure deficits become
critical. The mobile network operator datasets used in this study provide the
temporal resolution needed for this purpose; their integration into standard
planning workflows should be a priority for metropolitan transport authorities.
