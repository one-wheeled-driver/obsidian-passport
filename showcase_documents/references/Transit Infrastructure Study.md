# Urban Transit Infrastructure

A systematic capacity assessment of metropolitan transit networks across twelve
European cities, conducted between 2022 and 2024. The study combines automated
passenger-count data, infrastructure audit records, and investment ledger data
to produce a comprehensive picture of network health and forward investment needs.

---

## Methodology

### Data Collection

Ridership data was obtained from each city's transport authority covering a
five-year window (2019–2023). Automated passenger counting (APC) systems
provided per-station, per-hour resolution, with a data-completeness threshold
of 95 % required for inclusion in the main analysis; two cities were downgraded
to supplementary analysis on this basis.

Infrastructure records — track age, rolling-stock fleet lists, signalling
generation, and scheduled maintenance cycles — were obtained via freedom-of-
information requests where not publicly available.

### Capacity Model

Capacity was defined as the ratio of observed peak-hour passenger flow to the
design capacity of the infrastructure (track throughput × train length × average
headway). Values above 1.0 indicate saturation; values below 0.7 indicate spare
capacity that may be recoverable through demand management rather than capital
investment.

The model accounts for the fact that design capacity is a theoretical ceiling;
practical usable capacity is approximately 85 % of design capacity due to dwell-
time variability, uneven platform loading, and operational buffers.

---

## Key Findings

### Network Saturation

Across the twelve cities surveyed, **80 %** of metro lines recorded a peak-hour
load factor above 1.0 on at least three days per week. Seven of the twelve cities
had at least one line operating at or above 120 % of design capacity during peak
hours, indicating severe crowding by international service-quality benchmarks. ^saturation-finding

The saturation is temporally concentrated: 73 % of excess load occurs in a
90-minute window centred on 08:15. Off-peak load factors average 0.41, implying
that the network has substantial unused capacity for most of the operating day.

### Investment Gaps

Investment in new rolling stock has lagged behind ridership growth by an average
of 6.3 years across the sample. Only two cities had completed a full network audit
in the previous decade. The average age of rolling stock across the sample is
22 years, against a design life of 30–35 years, suggesting that a significant
replacement cycle is imminent regardless of ridership growth. ^investment-gap

Capital budgets for transit infrastructure have averaged 0.18 % of city GDP over
the study period — well below the 0.3 % threshold that our investment model
identifies as necessary to maintain current capacity ratios while absorbing
projected ridership growth.

### Cross-Modal Integration

Park-and-ride schemes show particularly weak integration. Fewer than 30 % of
stations offered covered bicycle parking, and real-time capacity information was
absent from most facilities. Interchange time penalties — the extra journey time
imposed by poor wayfinding, level changes, and crowding at interchange points —
average 4.7 minutes per interchange, equivalent to roughly 40 % of the typical
in-vehicle time for a two-leg commute. ^crossmodal-gap

## Recommendations

### Short-Term Actions

Immediate upgrades to signalling systems in saturated corridors would increase
theoretical capacity by 12–18 % without capital expenditure on new physical
infrastructure. Communication-based train control (CBTC) retrofits typically
recover this capacity within 18–24 months of commissioning.

Network operators should also explore demand-side interventions informed by the
nudge-theory literature — in particular the choice-architecture principles in
[[Nudge Theory Origins#Choice Architecture]] — to shift discretionary trips out
of the peak 90-minute window.

### Medium-Term Actions

A dedicated infrastructure fund, capitalised at 0.3 % of each city's GDP
annually, would close the investment gap within eight years according to the
model developed in this study. Procurement should be aggregated across cities
to achieve economies of scale in rolling-stock orders.

### Long-Term Vision

Integrated multi-modal hubs, informed by the migration patterns documented in
[[Population Migration Patterns#Urban Attractors]], offer the most resilient
long-term configuration for growing metropolitan areas. Hub design should
prioritise step-free access, covered cycle parking, and real-time information
displays integrated across all modes.
