# Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness

The foundational text establishing the policy framework of *libertarian
paternalism* — designing choice architectures that steer individuals toward
better outcomes while preserving freedom of choice. The book synthesises
prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979), dual-process cognition (Stanovich &
West, 2000), and applied mechanism design to produce an actionable policy toolkit
that has been adopted by governments in more than thirty countries.

---

## Core Principles

### Choice Architecture

Every choice environment has an architecture whether designers intend it or not.
Defaults, ordering, framing, physical layout, and feedback loops all shape
decisions systematically and predictably. Nudge theory argues that these effects
should be deployed deliberately in the public interest rather than left to
accumulate accidentally or be exploited commercially. ^choice-architecture

A choice architect is anyone who organises the context in which people make
decisions — transit operators setting fare structures, city planners locating
bicycle parking, or policymakers designing opt-in versus opt-out enrolment
systems. All of them are, whether they know it or not, choice architects.

### The Two-System Model

System 1 (fast, automatic, pattern-matching, associative) dominates most
everyday decisions, including routine commute choices. System 2 (slow,
deliberate, rule-following, analytical) is engaged only for novel or effortful
tasks. Effective nudges target System 1 responses — salience, social norms,
pre-commitment — without requiring conscious deliberation. ^two-system-model

The practical implication for transit policy is significant: a fare change that
requires a commuter to consciously recalculate journey cost every morning will
have a different — and generally smaller — behavioural effect than an equally
large change embedded in the structure of a monthly subscription, where System 1
simply records "already paid, free to travel."

### Loss Aversion

Losses loom larger than equivalent gains by a ratio of approximately **2.25:1**
(the seminal estimate from Kahneman & Tversky's 1979 prospect-theory paper,
subsequently replicated across hundreds of studies in diverse cultural and
economic contexts). Policies framed around avoiding losses are therefore more
motivating than those framed around acquiring equivalent gains. ^loss-aversion-ratio

In transit contexts this means that a fare *increase* of €0.50 is experienced as
more than twice as aversive as a fare *reduction* of €0.50 is experienced as
pleasurable — even though the net financial effect is identical. Any authority
considering fare restructuring should design the communication of that
restructuring around loss avoidance.

## Policy Applications

### Default Effects

Setting the desired behaviour as the default option is among the most
consistently effective nudges across all domains studied. The mechanism is
straightforward: most people accept the default rather than expend cognitive
resources on an active decision. Opt-out rates are typically **15–30 percentage
points lower** than opt-in rates for economically identical programmes.

Applied to transit: auto-enrolment in monthly pass schemes for employees whose
payroll systems already process commuter benefits has produced take-up rates of
78–91 % versus 35–52 % under voluntary enrolment in matched comparisons.

### Social Norm Messaging

Informing individuals that most of their peers already perform the desired
behaviour reliably increases uptake without financial incentives. Messages of the
form "8 out of 10 of your colleagues travel to work by public transit" increase
transit use among car commuters by 9–14 % in controlled field experiments,
even when the recipient knows intellectually that the statistic is simply
descriptive.

The effect is strongest when the reference group is specific and proximate
(colleagues in the same office rather than residents of the same city) and when
the message is delivered at the decision point rather than in advance.

### Commitment Devices

Pre-commitment mechanisms exploit *present bias* — the tendency to give
disproportionate weight to immediate costs and benefits relative to future ones.
By binding future behaviour to a present decision, commitment devices
substantially increase follow-through. ^commitment-devices

In transit contexts, "30-day transit pledge" campaigns — in which participants
publicly commit to using public transit for all commutes over a trial period —
have achieved sustained mode shifts of 18–27 % of participants beyond the pledge
period. The public commitment element (social accountability) amplifies the
effect relative to private-only pledges by approximately 40 %.

## Criticisms and Limitations

Libertarian paternalism has attracted criticism from both directions: libertarians
object that even non-coercive nudges limit autonomy through manipulation of the
decision environment; progressives argue that nudges are an inadequate substitute
for structural interventions (public investment, regulation) that address root
causes. The empirical record supports a middle position: nudges are most effective
as complements to, not substitutes for, structural change, and their effects are
typically modest in magnitude but cheap to implement and scalable.
