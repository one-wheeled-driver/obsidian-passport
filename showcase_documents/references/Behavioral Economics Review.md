# Behavioural Economics and Public Transport Uptake

A systematic review of the empirical literature on how cognitive biases and
decision-making heuristics influence mode choice and fare sensitivity, with
particular attention to practical levers available to transit operators and
public authorities. The review covers 147 peer-reviewed studies published
between 2005 and 2022.

The theoretical foundations — prospect theory, dual-process cognition, and
libertarian paternalism — are developed in [[Nudge Theory Origins]], which should
be read as a companion to this review.

---

## Loss Aversion and Transit

### The Framing Effect in Fare Design

Prospect theory predicts that passengers experience fare increases as losses and
fare reductions as foregone gains — with losses weighted roughly 2.25× more
heavily than equivalent gains. Empirical tests in Stockholm and Zurich confirm
this asymmetry in transit contexts: the same net price change produces
significantly different ridership responses depending on how it is framed.

The theoretical grounding for this effect — including the ratio of 2.25:1 between
the psychological weight of losses and gains — is formalised in
[[Nudge Theory Origins#^loss-aversion-ratio]].

### Penalty vs. Reward Framing

Peak-hour surcharges framed as "standard fare with off-peak discount" generated
**23 % higher off-peak ridership** than the economically equivalent "off-peak
fare with peak surcharge" in a controlled trial across 14 bus lines in Vienna
(n = 180 000 journeys over 12 months; p < 0.001). The effect was stable across
income quartiles but was approximately 35 % larger among riders who held a
monthly pass, consistent with the hypothesis that pass holders engage in more
deliberate loss-accounting than casual-fare riders.

## Decision Fatigue

### Complexity and Fare Systems

Overly complex fare structures increase the cognitive load imposed on riders at
the point of purchase or boarding. In a structured survey of 2 400 commuters
across six cities, riders facing more than four tariff zones reported
significantly lower satisfaction with the transit system and a 22 % higher
stated likelihood of switching to private-car use on days when journey cost was
uncertain — a decision-fatigue response. ^complexity-effect

Simplification of fare systems (reduction from zone-based to flat or two-tier
fares) in two UK cities was associated with a 7–11 % increase in casual-rider
patronage within 18 months of implementation, even with no change in absolute
fare levels.

### Subscription Effects

Monthly pass holders show markedly higher ridership and lower sensitivity to
short-term service disruptions than equivalent point-of-sale fare payers.
The mechanism is most plausibly a sunk-cost commitment effect: having already
paid for the month, the marginal cost of each trip is perceived as zero, which
suppresses car-use consideration and smooths demand throughout the week.

Targeted subscription campaigns — particularly those using the pre-commitment
architecture described in [[Nudge Theory Origins#^commitment-devices]] — have
achieved net new pass take-up rates of 12–18 % among previously irregular riders
in field experiments in Amsterdam, Lyon, and Helsinki.

## Fare Elasticity

### Price Sensitivity Estimates

A meta-analysis of 47 studies conducted between 2005 and 2022 yields a mean
short-run own-price elasticity of demand of **−0.38** (95 % CI: −0.42, −0.34)
for urban transit. Long-run elasticity reaches −0.65, indicating that initial
ridership losses from fare increases are substantially amplified over time as
commuters invest in alternatives (car purchase, residential relocation). ^fare-elasticity-block

> [!IMPORTANT] Long-run elasticity is the planning-relevant figure
> Short-run estimates underestimate the true behavioural response by roughly a
> factor of two. Fare-policy decisions made on short-run elasticity alone will
> systematically underpredict ridership loss from price increases — and
> ridership gain from sustained discounts.

The long-run elasticity is meaningfully larger in cities where congestion charges
or parking levies create a competing cost for private car use, suggesting that
fare and car-access policy should be designed jointly.

### Income Heterogeneity

Price sensitivity is significantly higher among low-income riders. A 10 % fare
increase reduces ridership by **6.1 %** in the lowest income quartile but only
**2.9 %** in the highest quartile, with important distributional implications for
flat-rate fare policies. Means-tested fare discounts preserve both ridership
levels and social equity better than across-the-board flat-rate reductions of
equivalent fiscal cost.

### Cross-Modal Price Effects

The own-price elasticity of transit is highly sensitive to the availability of
alternatives. In cities where car use is cheap and parking is plentiful, the
cross-price elasticity between transit and private car is estimated at +0.15 to
+0.25 — meaning that transit fare increases cause non-trivial shifts to car use.
Integrated mobility pricing (joint optimisation of transit fares, congestion
charges, and parking levies) is consistently more effective than single-lever
interventions.
